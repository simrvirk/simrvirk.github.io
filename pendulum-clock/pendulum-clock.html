The Monolith —
Mechanical Pendulum Clock

Designed, manufactured, assembled, and commissioned a fully functional weight-driven mechanical pendulum wall clock from the ground up — 3D-printed on a Bambu P1S in matte black PLA (frame) and matte white PLA (gears). Original architectural design: tall vertical form factor, exposed spoked gear train, Graham deadbeat escapement, floating wall standoffs. Frame and all structural elements designed in SolidWorks with a shared master layout sketch. Runs for multiple days on a single wind; failure modes diagnosed and resolved via systematic root-cause analysis.

Frame Design & Mechanical Architecture
  • Two-plate construction — front and back frame plates connected by pillars; both plates driven by a single master layout sketch in SolidWorks so bearing hole positions propagate simultaneously across the assembly without manual synchronization
  • Cantilever deflection eliminated — identified tip deflection of cantilevered escapement arbors as a critical failure mode: center-to-center spacing shifts beyond cycloidal gear tolerance under load, causing escapement lockup; resolved via a partial-ring front plate around the escapement that provides front-end arbor support while preserving the skeletal aesthetic
  • Frame split at 255 mm — frame height exceeded the Bambu P1S 256 mm print bed; split into two interlocking pieces with an angled joint secured by screws; pure press-fit rejected because PLA creeps under sustained gravity-induced shear load
  • 623RS bearing bosses (3 × 10 × 4 mm) designed as interference fits; tightest tolerances applied at the escapement arbor and pendulum pivot — highest-leverage positions for friction reduction
  • Tolerance stack-up — Z-axis gear train depth calculated from caliper-measured printed gear thicknesses, not nominal values; spacer lengths set to produce <0.5 mm end shake at each arbor
  • Print process control — 0.15 mm layer height, 4 walls, Classic perimeter generator (Arachne rejected — varied wall count at gear tooth tips weakened tooth geometry), 0.25 mm elephant foot compensation, random seam to prevent repeating seam line coinciding with the same tooth position each revolution
  • Norse mythology motifs on front plate — deliberate design element engraved into the front frame face; a personal timestamp marking what was happening during the build (the God of War series). The clock is meant to document not just the engineering but the moment in time it was made.
Gear Train, Escapement & Drive System
  • SP5B gear train (Steve Peterson) adopted as a verified subsystem — module 1.86765 mm (13.6 DP), cycloidal tooth profiles; gear train rotated 90° counter-clockwise from original horizontal layout to achieve the tall vertical "Monolith" form factor
  • Constant tooth sum of 66 across all SP5B runtime configurations → fixed center distance of 61.63 mm regardless of gear count combination; independently verified total reduction ratio confirming ~86,400 escapement impulses/day at seconds-beat
  • Cycloidal geometry constraint — operating center distance must be verified from printed parts, not derived theoretically; this drove the decision to use Steve's STLs unchanged rather than redesigning gear geometry
  • Graham deadbeat escapement — escape wheel reprinted at 0.12 mm layer height to improve impulse face surface finish and minimize pallet-face drag; smoother 623RS bearings reserved for this arbor
  • Block-and-tackle drive — movable pulley in weight shell halves effective drum force (minimum ~3.2 lbs at drum → minimum 6.4 lbs total weight required); doubles runtime and doubles cord paid out per drum revolution; 65 lb braided PowerPro fishing line, figure-8 knot anchored to G8 spoke
  • Friction clutch (hand-setting) — pen-spring clutch between driven gear (loose on arbor) and pinion (locked via set screw); transmits running torque, slips under hand-applied time-setting force; minute hand hub uses D-profile bore matched to filed flat on 3 mm arbor — no keyway broach required
Commissioning & Failure Mode Resolution
  • Failure Mode 1 — Pendulum amplitude dying: drive cord contacting adjacent frame members introduced cord friction that reduced effective drum torque below the escapement's minimum self-sustaining threshold; fixed by adjusting cord path geometry to eliminate frame interference
  • Failure Mode 2 — Gear train lockup: repeatable stall (not random) indicated geometric lockup rather than torque deficiency; isolated by removing pallet — gear train moved freely, confirming escapement as root cause; fixed by rotating pallet body to increase arm engagement depth and reprinting escape wheel at 0.12 mm
  • Weight calibration: operating weight set at 1.5× identified minimum self-sustaining threshold; weight shell sized and filled (BB shot) to achieve target with margin
  • Dropped weight incident: 10 lb filled shell fell from ~6 ft during cord routing — a practical reminder that a drive weight at full drop height stores ~81 ft·lbs of potential energy and is a genuine impact hazard; foot confirmed not load-bearing during clock assembly going forward
  • Print failure root cause: partial nozzle clog compounded into blob-of-death event — traced to symptom chasing (re-printing) rather than eliminating root cause; nozzle replaced, filament dried at 120°F / 5 hours in dehydrator to address moisture-related stringing
Manufacturing & Assembly
  • Shaft preparation — 3 mm stainless steel arbor rod cut with bi-metal hacksaw (18–24 TPI), ends deburred and sanded to 623RS inner bore; 1.5 mm music wire (hardened spring steel) for pallet arbor, cut with abrasive disc; D-flat filed with needle file set, depth verified by calipers
  • Bearing preparation — all 623RS bearings flushed of factory grease via overnight IPA soak, dried, and spin-tested; smoothest bearings reserved for escapement arbor and pendulum pivot; lightweight oil applied to pendulum pivot for long-term wear protection
  • Assembly sequence — bearings pressed in → gears + spacers on arbors → drive cord → pallet + pendulum installed last, after gear train confirmed free under applied load
  • Lubrication — trace grease on pallet faces and pinion leaves only; no grease on large gear wheel teeth (attracts abrasive dust)
  • SolidWorks Design Tables — managed multiple gear configurations (varying tooth and spoke counts) within a single part file; feature suppression columns handle spoke geometry that would violate wall thickness minimums at low tooth counts
