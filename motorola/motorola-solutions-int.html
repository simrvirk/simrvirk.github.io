Overview

At Motorola Solutions' Avigilon division in Vancouver, I completed two consecutive co-op work terms (September 2025 – April 2026) embedded within the Design Validation Team (DVT). The DVT is the gatekeeping function between engineering and mass production — every camera product that ships has passed through the processes this team owns. The role demanded fluency across mechanical systems, electronics, firmware behaviour, optics, and software automation, often in the same week.

During my first term I functioned as a full member of the DVT, performing temperature and environmental stress testing on new camera designs, conducting optical and image quality evaluations, executing hardware functionality assessments, and carrying out failure investigations with root cause analysis. I collaborated directly with mechanical, electrical, and firmware engineering teams to reproduce issues, characterize their source, and validate that proposed fixes resolved them — the full DVT loop. I also developed scripted test automation to reduce manual burden on repetitive procedures.

During my second term I was given ownership of a harder problem: replace the DVT's manual, trigonometry-based Field of View measurement method with something automated, accurate, and scalable. I conceived, designed, and built a fully automated FOV Goniometer system — a Python and OpenCV pipeline integrated with a Zaber motorized rotary stage that measures camera FOV directly via angular encoder feedback. The system achieved ±0.5° measurement accuracy against the Trioptics ImageMaster optical bench (a commercially available precision instrument valued at a minimum of $40,000 USD) and reduced test cycle time by 85%, exceeding the 50% reduction target set at project kickoff. It is now part of the lab's permanent validation infrastructure, operable by any engineering staff member with zero optical training required.

What I Built

Key Contributions

01 — Flagship · Term 2
Automated FOV Goniometer System
Designed and built an automated Field of View measurement system from the ground up, replacing the manual flat-wall trigonometry method the DVT had been using. The system uses a Zaber motorized rotary stage to rotate a green laser line while a Python/OpenCV pipeline streams live RTSP video from the camera under test (DUT). FOV is measured directly via the motor's internal rotary encoder — the physical standoff distance is mathematically irrelevant, eliminating the dominant error source of the old approach. Custom SolidWorks-designed, 3D-printed mounts integrated the laser and camera precisely on the stage axis. Achieved ±0.5° accuracy versus the Trioptics ImageMaster optical bench ($40,000+ instrument) with 85% reduction in test cycle time.
02 — Computer Vision · Term 2
RTSP Pipeline & Single-Channel Thresholding
Built a real-time Python/OpenCV video processing pipeline that ingests live RTSP streams from cameras under test without any proprietary software integration. Initial RGB channel processing proved unstable at wide-angle edges due to chromatic aberration causing color fringing across adjacent pixel columns, corrupting laser boundary detection. Redesigned the pipeline to extract only the raw green grayscale luminance channel and apply strict binary thresholding, producing the detection mask. This single-channel approach yielded sharper, faster, more consistent edge detection and proved immune to chromatic aberration — a non-obvious fix that required understanding both the optics and the software.
03 — Architecture · Term 2
Discrete Step Engine (DSE)
Initial motor control used continuous velocity sweep commands, which produced asynchronous TCP buffer collisions due to network latency — causing the Zaber controller to drop connections mid-sweep and corrupt angular readings. Replaced the architecture with a Discrete Step Engine: the motor advances in fixed 0.05° increments, comes to a complete mechanical stop, flushes the network buffer, then evaluates the current video frame before advancing. This eliminated all connection failures and guaranteed that each encoder position report was synchronized with a corresponding captured video frame. Clean architecture that future engineers can maintain and extend.
04 — Optical Metrology · Term 2
Advanced Error Correction Suite
Identified and corrected three independent systematic error sources that prevented the system from meeting the ±0.5° accuracy target. Finite beam error: the laser's non-zero angular width artificially inflated raw FOV readings — resolved via an automated pre-sweep calibration that dynamically profiles and subtracts beam angular width per session. Focus breathing: contrast-detect autofocus hunting in a darkened environment caused frame-to-frame focal length drift — resolved by introducing controlled ambient lighting and high-contrast reference targets. Entrance pupil parallax: rotating about the front lens rather than the nodal point introduced up to 2.2° of geometric parallax error — root cause identified; proposed macro rail calibration procedure for future elimination.
05 — Environmental Testing · Term 1
Camera Validation & Environmental Stress Testing
As a full member of the DVT during my first term, I performed temperature and environmental stress testing on new camera designs across multiple product lines — High/Low Temperature, Accelerated Life Tests (ALT), condensation cycling, and cold-start verification, all within dedicated climate chambers. I also conducted optical and image quality baselining (FOV, occlusion, vignetting), camera functionality evaluation, and assessments of lens and optical element performance. Developed scripted test automation using Python and Bash to reduce manual burden on high-repetition procedures, consistent with the team's push-button operation philosophy.
06 — Cross-Team Collaboration · Term 1
Failure Investigation, Root Cause Analysis & FAI
Performed failure investigations and root cause analysis on pre-production hardware in collaboration with mechanical, electrical, and firmware engineering teams — the full DVT loop from issue reproduction to fix validation. Carried out First Article Inspections (FAI) on new production units to verify conformance before release. Findings were documented in structured test reports and escalated through the appropriate engineering channels. Working at this intersection — between hardware, firmware, and manufacturing — reinforced that the most effective validation engineers understand enough of each discipline to ask the right question before they start running tests.
Deep Dive — Term 2

FOV Goniometer
Automation System

The problem. The DVT's standard FOV measurement relied on flat-wall trigonometry: position a camera at a known standoff distance, photograph a marked wall, and calculate the FOV using FOV = 2 × arctan(BC / 2AD). This suffered from three compounding deficiencies: at a 50 cm standoff, a 1 cm distance measurement error introduced a 1.3% FOV error; the method was geometrically invalid for fisheye and wide-angle lenses due to their nonlinear distortion; and manual operator edge-identification introduced per-unit variance that made repeatability impossible to certify.

The architecture. I replaced linear distance measurement with direct angular encoder feedback. A Zaber motorized rotary stage serves as the precision platform. A green laser line is mounted on the stage and rotated while the camera under test (DUT) observes from a fixed position on the rotation axis. Python-based control software monitors the camera's RTSP stream and records the precise encoder angle at the moment the laser reaches each extreme edge of the camera frame. The FOV is the angular difference between those two positions. Because the measurement relies exclusively on the motor's internal rotary encoder, the physical standoff distance is mathematically irrelevant — the dominant error source of the old method is structurally eliminated.

The Discrete Step Engine. Continuous velocity sweep commands initially caused asynchronous TCP buffer collisions due to Zaber network latency, dropping motor controller connections during active sweeps. I replaced this with a DSE: the motor advances in fixed 0.05° increments, comes to a complete mechanical stop, flushes the TCP network buffer, then evaluates the current video frame before issuing the next move command. Every reported encoder angle is guaranteed to correspond to its matching video frame — no interpolation, no drift.

The error correction suite. Three independent systematic error sources required targeted solutions before the system could meet the ±0.5° target. First, chromatic aberration at wide-angle edges caused RGB-based laser detection to fail — solved by switching to single green-channel binary thresholding. Second, finite beam width inflated raw FOV readings because the detector triggered on the leading edge of one side and the trailing edge of the other — solved via an automated pre-sweep center-frame calibration that dynamically measures and subtracts beam angular width for each test session. Third, focus breathing in fully darkened environments caused contrast-detect autofocus to hunt between frames, introducing trial-to-trial focal length variance — solved by introducing controlled ambient lighting and high-contrast reference targets to stabilize the autofocus lock position.

Remaining work — nodal point parallax. Initial prototype testing revealed FOV errors up to 2.2° at full-wide zoom, traceable to entrance pupil parallax: the camera's nodal point was not co-located with the rotary stage axis. After all other corrections, residual error reduced to approximately 0.5°. I proposed and designed a macro rail (or Zaber linear actuator) nodal point calibration procedure — a foreground-background alignment test where near-field and far-field targets are observed simultaneously while the camera translates along the optical axis until neither target shows lateral displacement during stage rotation. This centers the nodal point precisely over the rotation axis and projects elimination of the remaining parallax artifact.
