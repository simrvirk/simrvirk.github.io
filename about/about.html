Who I Am

Connecting mechanics,
electronics, and code.

I'm Simr Virk, a Mechatronic Systems Engineering student at Simon Fraser University, graduating in 2027. My discipline lives at the intersection of mechanical design, embedded electronics, and software — and I believe engineers who are fluent in all three build things that engineers who aren't, simply can't.

From writing firmware at Motorola Solutions for mission-critical public safety hardware, to designing a 5-DOF vision-guided robot arm from scratch, I gravitate toward problems where precision is non-negotiable and the margin for error is tight. That's where I do my best work.

Outside the technical work, I'm driven by a broader question: what makes a system elegant? Whether it's a gear train, a control loop, or a team — I'm always thinking about how the parts fit together. I'm actively seeking co-op and full-time roles in robotics, autonomous systems, or embedded hardware-software integration where I can build things that matter at scale.

Beyond the Lab

Life outside
the work.

The engineers I admire most aren't one-dimensional. Curiosity doesn't clock out — it follows you to the bookshelf, the golf course, the trail, and across time zones. The best ideas I've brought into my engineering work didn't come from textbooks. They came from everywhere else. Here's what keeps me sharp, grounded, and thinking differently.

01 — Reading

What I'm
Reading

Engineering sharpened my ability to solve problems. Books sharpened my ability to think about them. I read across biography, systems thinking, business history, and the philosophy of technology — because the best engineers borrow ideas from everywhere. If I can't explain a concept in plain language, I don't fully understand it yet.

BiographySystems Thinking Business HistoryPhilosophy of Technology
02 — Golf

On the
Course

Golf is 10% swing and 90% systems thinking. Every shot is a feedback loop: you set a target, execute, measure the deviation, and adjust. That cycle — precision, patience, and iteration — is identical to engineering a control system. I've been chasing a clean round since high school, and I still haven't found it. That's exactly why I keep going back.

PrecisionMental Discipline Feedback LoopsIteration
03 — Travel

Exploring
the World

Perspective is one of the most underrated engineering tools. Stepping into a new city — navigating a different transit system, reading a different culture, solving a different set of daily frictions — rewires how you think about design. Every place I've visited has quietly changed how I approach problems back home. The world is the widest requirements document ever written.

Cultural CuriosityAdaptabilityPerspective
04 — Outdoors

Getting
Outside

Living in British Columbia means the outdoors is never more than 30 minutes away. Hiking, skiing, and exploring BC's trails are how I decompress and reset. Time outside makes the time inside the lab more focused — you can't debug at full capacity if you never step away from the bench. Some of the best engineering in the world has no power button, and I never get tired of reminding myself of that.

06 — Creative Builds

Building in
Virtual Worlds

Before I had a machine shop or an oscilloscope, I had Roblox Studio. Building in virtual environments — designing systems with scripted logic, spatial constraints, and real user interaction — was my earliest exposure to iterative design and systems thinking. Having to make something people actually want to use, without being able to blame the physics, teaches you to think from the user's perspective first. It's a habit I carry into every project.

08 — Anime & Manga

Illustrated
Stories

I gravitate toward anime and manga with serious world-building and high-stakes narratives — the kind that make you think about something long after you've finished it. Less shonen power-scaling, more thematic weight. The list below leans toward titles that either redefined the medium or made me genuinely reconsider something. The slider cycles through cover art automatically.

Shonen Seinen World-Building High-Stakes Narrative
  • Attack on Titan
    The most complete narrative in anime — every detail in season 1 pays off years later. The finale is divisive but the journey is unmatched.
  • Vinland Saga
    A slow-burn deconstruction of revenge and violence. Thorfinn's arc from Season 1 to Season 2 is the best character growth in the medium.
  • Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood
    Perfectly paced, thematically rich, and emotionally devastating. The alchemy rules are tight enough to be genuinely satisfying as a systems-thinker.
  • Berserk (Manga)
    Kentaro Miura's masterwork. The Golden Age arc is a tragedy in the classical sense — and the art is some of the most detailed ever drawn.
  • Death Note
    A chess match stretched into 37 episodes. The cat-and-mouse dynamic between Light and L is still the sharpest writing in anime.
